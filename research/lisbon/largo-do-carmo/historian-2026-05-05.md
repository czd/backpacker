## Topic A — 1755 Lisbon earthquake & the Carmo

### Era / date precision

**1 November 1755 — All Saints' Day (*Dia de Todos-os-Santos*).** Three shocks; the first hit at approximately **09:40 local time**. The religious context is load-bearing for the death-toll geography: it was a Catholic holy day of obligation, congregations were gathered for morning Mass, candles were lit on every altar in the city. When roofs came down, they came down on full churches. When the candles fell, they ignited the wreckage. **Confidence: well-documented** — the time is from contemporary survivor accounts (notably the Jesuit Manuel Portal, the British merchant Thomas Chase, and Voltaire's secondhand sources), corroborated by clock-stoppage records.

### Magnitude and the geography of damage

- **Magnitude ~8.5–9.0 Mw** by modern reconstruction (Gutenberg-Richter retrofit; the 1755 event is among the most destructive seismic events in European history). **Confidence: scholarly consensus** for the magnitude range, with active debate at the upper bound. The epicenter was offshore on the Azores–Gibraltar fault system, ~200 km southwest of Cape St. Vincent.
- **Three-way disaster:** the earthquake itself (~10 minutes total ground motion across three shocks) → **tsunami** (~6m wave height at the Tagus mouth ~30–40 minutes after the first shock, sweeping the open *Terreiro do Paço* riverfront where survivors had fled to escape collapsing buildings) → **fire** (the fallen candles ignited the wreckage; the fire burned for **5–6 days**, completing what the quake started). Each phase compounded the previous.
- **Death toll:** modern scholarly consensus is **30,000–50,000 dead in Lisbon proper** out of a pre-quake population of ~200,000. Older estimates ran as high as 100,000 but are now considered inflated; the lower end (~10,000) is too low because it omits secondary fire deaths. **Confidence: active scholarly debate** within the 30k–50k window; "tens of thousands" is the cozy-honest framing.
- **Geography of damage:** the Baixa (the low river-front grid) was nearly totally destroyed. The hilltops fared mixed — Bairro Alto and Alfama partially survived (Alfama's Moorish-era density on bedrock fared better than the Baixa's reclaimed-marsh substrate, which liquefied). **The Carmo, sitting on the western edge of the Chiado plateau directly above the Baixa, was structurally devastated.** Belém (5 km west) was largely spared because of geographic distance from the worst ground motion. Madre de Deus (east, on a different geological substrate) survived — which is why the azulejo Master Cycles there are intact.

### Convento do Carmo specifically

- **Foundation:** 1389 by **Dom Nuno Álvares Pereira** (1360–1431), the *Condestável* (Constable) of Portugal under João I. After his decisive military leadership at the **Battle of Aljubarrota (1385)** — the battle that secured Portugal's independence from Castile — Nuno Álvares retreated from public life, founded the convent for the Carmelite Order, and eventually entered it himself as Brother Nuno de Santa Maria. He died there in 1431. He was beatified in 1918 and **canonized as a saint by Benedict XVI on 26 April 2009**. His tomb was originally in the convent church; relocated within the structure post-1755. **Confidence: well-documented** for the foundation and the Aljubarrota link; **scholarly consensus** for the canonization sequence.
- **Pre-quake church:** the **largest Gothic church in Lisbon** — five-nave plan, ~70m long, the Carmelite Order's principal house in Portugal. The library was reputedly one of the great Iberian collections of the 17th–18th c. (largely destroyed in the quake-fire).
- **The 1755 collapse during All Saints' Day Mass** is the load-bearing image. The roof — Gothic ribbed vaulting on stone piers — fell into the nave during the service. The exact death toll inside the Carmo specifically is not well-documented (no surviving parish register from the day), but contemporary accounts describe the convent as among the most catastrophic single-building losses in central Lisbon. **The roofless nave the player sees today is the literal preserved scar of that morning.** That is not architectural metaphor — it is what failed to be rebuilt.
- **Post-quake reconstruction abandonment:** Pombal (the Marquês de Pombal — *Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo*, the king's chief minister who ran the reconstruction) initiated rebuilding at the Carmo in the 1760s. Reconstruction was abandoned within ~a decade for two reasons: **economic** (the Pombaline reconstruction of the Baixa absorbed all available labor and capital — the new grid-plan downtown was the priority), and **ideological** (Pombal's Enlightenment-modernizing program was institutionally hostile to the older religious orders; he expelled the Jesuits in 1759 and viewed Carmelite reconstruction as a low priority). The roofless skeleton has stood **since c. 1770** — for ~256 years as of 2026. **Confidence: scholarly consensus** for the abandonment cause (the Pombal-vs-Carmelite ideological angle is more textured in scholarship than I'm flattening here, but the material outcome is uncontested).
- **Today:** the surviving structure (the apse, the side chapels, the surviving lower walls, the facade) houses the **Museu Arqueológico do Carmo** (Archaeological Museum of the Carmo), founded **1864** by the *Associação dos Arqueólogos Portugueses* — making it one of Portugal's oldest museums. The museum's collection includes prehistoric and Roman material, **two Peruvian pre-Columbian mummies** (a curatorial coincidence of 19th-c. collection-building, not a historical Lisbon connection — worth flagging because a player might tap on this and wonder), medieval sarcophagi, and **the tomb of King Ferdinand I of Portugal** (*D. Fernando I*, r. 1367–1383, relocated from elsewhere). The Nuno Álvares Pereira tomb is at the convent in some accounts; others place his canonized relics in the *Igreja de São Vicente de Fora*. I'd verify before any in-game claim — **flag for FD** to confirm via Museu Arqueológico do Carmo's public materials before any description sentence makes a tomb claim.

### What the player physically sees

Standing in **Largo do Carmo** (the square, not the ruin):

- The **west-facing façade** of the Carmo church — Gothic, rose window, pointed arch portal. The façade is structurally largely intact; the roof and most of the nave behind it are not.
- Inside (museum entry, ticketed) — the **Gothic nave open to the sky**. Pillars and ribbed arch springs against blue (or grey, or rain). The visual is unforgettable and unique in Lisbon. Pre-Columbian Peruvian mummies, Roman lapidary, medieval tomb effigies.
- The square itself: **plane trees** (*plátanos*, *Platanus × acerifolia*) — mature, providing shade. The Lisboeta term *largo* is "wider than a *praça*, less formal" — a square that's a neighborhood-living-room, not a monument-frame.
- **Chafariz do Carmo** — the **18th-c. fountain** at the center of the square. Built in 1771 (post-quake, Pombaline-era), one of the public fountains that supplied water to upper Lisbon before mains plumbing. Still flows. Octagonal basin, central column with masks. **Confidence: well-documented**.
- The **Quartel do Carmo** — the working **GNR (Guarda Nacional Republicana) barracks** on the south side of the square. This is Topic B's anchor; flagging it here because the player sees it the same moment they see the church ruin.
- The **Elevador de Santa Justa** — the upper exit. Yes, that's right above the square (Topic D below).
- *On busking days:* musicians in the shade of the plane trees, tourists arriving via Santa Justa or the Chiado-up-from-Baixa walk, residents sitting at café tables.

### Pombaline reconstruction context

This was scaffolded in PR7's discovery (the §9.3 azulejo report). Re-anchoring for PR8:

- **Pombal's grid below, the Carmo skeleton above.** The Baixa Pombalina (the rebuilt downtown — Rua Augusta, Praça do Rossio, Praça do Comércio frontage) was **rebuilt 1758–1775** on a strict orthogonal grid by engineers Manuel da Maia, Carlos Mardel, and Eugénio dos Santos. The buildings used the *gaiola pombalina* — a wooden-cage anti-seismic frame embedded in the masonry, an extraordinary 18th-c. innovation. **Confidence: well-documented** — the *gaiola* is one of the best-studied early earthquake-resistant building systems in world history.
- **What was rebuilt vs what was left as ruin is itself a 1755 narrative.** The Baixa was rebuilt because it was the commercial heart and the king (José I) wanted it. The Carmo was abandoned because Pombal didn't want a Carmelite revival. The geography of post-1755 Lisbon — what stands and what is ruin — is **a political map of Pombal's priorities**, not just a record of damage. This is worth flagging for the M3+ NPC dialogue: a Lisboeta might say something like "the Marquês built the Baixa; he didn't build the Carmo." That's not anti-Pombal sentiment — it's just historical literacy.

### Plaques and signage at the site

What does the player physically encounter at the site? Itemizing for §9.3's "learning is a side effect of presence" and Topic D below:

- **Museu Arqueológico do Carmo entrance plaque** (south side of the church, off Largo do Carmo) — Portuguese-language: *"Museu Arqueológico do Carmo / Associação dos Arqueólogos Portugueses / Fundado em 1864"*. Hours and ticket info also there.
- **Convent foundation plaque** (often on the church façade) — references *Dom Nuno Álvares Pereira* and 1389. Exact wording varies by which historical-protection campaign added it; I don't have the current text verbatim. **Flag for FD**: if the description ever quotes plaque text, it should be verified against current site photos (Wikimedia, Mapillary).
- **Chafariz do Carmo** — typically carries a date plaque or carved stonework with the Pombaline-era date (1771).
- **GNR Quartel do Carmo plaque(s)** — Topic B below.

## Topic B — 25 de Abril 1974 / Carnation Revolution

### Estado Novo regime context

The dictatorship the Revolution ended:

- **Founded 1933** under António de Oliveira Salazar's *Constituição* (Constitution). Salazar — economics professor turned finance minister (1928) turned prime minister (1932) — built the *Estado Novo* on Catholic corporatism, ruralist nostalgia, and *"Deus, Pátria, Família"* ("God, Fatherland, Family"). The regime was **41 years old** by 1974 — making it longer-lived than Franco's Spain at the same point.
- **Salazar's stroke in August 1968** incapacitated him; **Marcelo Caetano** succeeded as prime minister (Salazar lived on, formally still convinced he was governing, until his death in July 1970 — the famous detail that the household maintained the fiction). Caetano's *primavera marcelista* ("Marcelist spring") of 1968–1970 was a brief liberalization that hardened back into authoritarianism by the early 1970s.
- **PIDE — *Polícia Internacional e de Defesa do Estado*** — the secret police, surfaced in M1 PR1's Aeroporto Humberto Delgado description. Renamed **DGS — *Direcção-Geral de Segurança*** in 1969 under Caetano, but every Lisboeta still called it PIDE. Operated through informers, surveillance, and a network of political prisons (*Caxias*, *Peniche*, *Tarrafal* in Cape Verde). Assassinated General Humberto Delgado on 13 February 1965.
- **The Colonial War (*Guerra Colonial*):** 1961–1974. Portugal fought three simultaneous wars to retain its African colonies — **Angola** (war began February 1961 with the *Baixa de Cassanje* uprising), **Guinea-Bissau** (war began January 1963), **Mozambique** (war began September 1964). By 1974 the wars were militarily unwinnable and economically ruinous. ~9,000 Portuguese soldiers killed; conscription of two-year service for every able-bodied young man; an entire generation of Portuguese men shaped by the wars. The wars are why the **MFA (*Movimento das Forças Armadas*) — the secret organization of mid-rank officers** (captains, majors, lieutenant-colonels) who planned the coup — existed: they were professional soldiers who had concluded the war could not be won and the regime had to end. The generals, by and large, were Estado Novo loyalists; the captains made the revolution. **Confidence: scholarly consensus** for all of this. The standard reference is Kenneth Maxwell's *The Making of Portuguese Democracy* (Cambridge UP, 1995) and António Costa Pinto's body of work in Portuguese.

### The morning of 25 April 1974

The play-by-play, abbreviated:

- **Two coordinated radio signals.** **22:55, 24 April:** Rádio Renascença plays *"E Depois do Adeus"* by **Paulo de Carvalho** — the song Portugal had submitted to the Eurovision Song Contest weeks before. This was the first signal: MFA units begin moving toward staging positions. **00:20, 25 April:** Rádio Renascença plays **Zeca Afonso's *"Grândola Vila Morena"*** — a song banned by the regime as crypto-communist (it celebrates a small Alentejo town's egalitarian community spirit). This was the second signal: full mobilization begins. **Confidence: well-documented**, the radio recordings exist and the MFA's signal protocol is established in the historical record.
- **Forces moved on Lisbon overnight.** **Salgueiro Maia** (*Fernando José Salgueiro Maia*, 1944–1992) — a young captain commanding the **Escola Prática de Cavalaria (EPC)** unit from **Santarém** — drove armored cars 80 km southwest into Lisbon. Other MFA units took the airport, the radio stations, key bridges. By morning the city was in MFA hands.
- **The siege of Quartel do Carmo.** Caetano had taken refuge at the **Quartel do Carmo** — **the GNR headquarters on Largo do Carmo** — early in the morning, recognizing the regime had fallen. Salgueiro Maia commanded the besieging MFA force in the square. Standoff lasted hours; civilians began arriving in the square in defiance of MFA orders to stay clear. **Around 17:30**, Caetano surrendered. He insisted on surrendering specifically to **General António de Spínola** (whose recent book *Portugal e o Futuro* had publicly advocated decolonization and triggered the regime's spring crisis) — Caetano wanted, essentially, dignity in the form of a general's hand to take his power, not a captain's. Spínola arrived; Caetano surrendered; Caetano was flown to Madeira and then Brazil into exile. **Largo do Carmo is the literal site of the regime's surrender.** **Confidence: well-documented** — eyewitness accounts, photographs, RTP archive footage all extant.
- **The carnations.** The detail every Lisboeta knows. **Celeste Caeiro**, a worker at the *Restaurante Sir* (or *Self-Service Sir*, depending on source — a self-service restaurant on the Rua Braancamp) was given a stock of red carnations to distribute to customers for the restaurant's first anniversary. The restaurant closed for the day because of the unfolding revolution. Celeste, walking with the unsold carnations, met soldiers in the **Rossio area** mid-morning. A soldier asked for a cigarette; she had none, gave him a carnation; he put it in his rifle barrel. Other soldiers asked for one. By mid-afternoon, photographs of carnation-stemmed rifles were circulating. **By evening, the international press had named it "the Carnation Revolution" (*Revolução dos Cravos*)**. The flower was not planned; the symbol emerged from one woman's leftover stock. **Confidence: well-documented** — Celeste Caeiro is still alive (born 1933, lived in Lisbon as of recent reporting), interviews are extant, the moment is one of Portugal's defining national stories. **Honest pushback on a temptation:** do not romanticize this into "and the people put flowers in rifles" — the people came out *because* the soldiers were already there with the carnations. The carnation came first, the popular involvement followed. The flower symbolizes the bloodless coup (only four civilians died, all shot by PIDE agents at the *Quartel-General da PIDE* on Rua António Maria Cardoso when crowds confronted them — the only deaths of the day were victims of the dying regime, not of the new one).
- **The verdict by sundown:** the regime had fallen; censorship was lifted; political prisoners were freed (notably from Caxias prison); the MFA's *Programa* was published.

### The carnations themselves and Largo do Carmo today

- The Quartel do Carmo is **still operational** as the **GNR (Guarda Nacional Republicana) Lisbon command headquarters**. Same building, continuous function, different regime.
- **There is a plaque** on the Quartel do Carmo façade — typically Portuguese-language, commemorating the 25 April 1974 events and the location's role. The wording varies by which round-anniversary commemorative campaign added or refreshed it; I don't have current text verbatim. **Flag for FD**: verify via current Mapillary / Wikimedia photos before any description references plaque content directly.
- **Carnations on the gate**: cyclically present, especially around 25 April every year (the national holiday — *Dia da Liberdade*, "Freedom Day"). Round anniversaries (25, 30, 40, 50 years) bring large public gatherings to the square. The 50th anniversary on **25 April 2024** was a major national event. **Confidence: well-documented** for the cyclical anniversary pattern; I'd describe the carnations as "often present" rather than "always present" — they fade, get cleared, get re-pinned by new visitors. The Anthropologist's parallel review will have a stronger feel for the day-to-day baseline ("it's anniversary week" vs "it's a Tuesday in October").

### Decolonization aftermath

- The **Programa do MFA** (the MFA's published program, released 26 April 1974) included three demands: democratic constitutional rule, immediate cessation of colonial wars, and economic-social development. The most consequential was the second.
- **Decolonization 1974–1975:** Guinea-Bissau (independence recognized 10 September 1974, having declared unilaterally in 1973), Mozambique (25 June 1975), Cape Verde (5 July 1975), São Tomé and Príncipe (12 July 1975), Angola (11 November 1975), East Timor (also briefly independent in November 1975, then immediately invaded by Indonesia — a separate tragedy). **Confidence: well-documented** dates.
- **The *retornados*:** approximately **500,000 Portuguese citizens** returned from former colonies in 1975–76, principally from Angola and Mozambique. Lisbon's population grew by nearly 10% in 18 months. The *retornados* were not all "settlers" in the European-colonial sense — many were second- or third-generation Lusophone Africans of mixed background, born in the colonies, who held Portuguese citizenship and now had no other home. The arrival reshaped Lisbon demographically, economically (housing crisis, labor surplus), and culturally (the food, the music, the everyday register of the city absorbed colonial-Lusophone content).
- **The colonial-legacy ADR (AGENTS.md §15)** — see Topic E.

## Topic C — Dual-layer description framing

### Description register recommendation

The PR7 dispatch and the M1 PR1 retroactive synthesis both surfaced the same lesson: **descriptions in this game are not enumerated facts; they are observations.** The player taps a POI marker and reads a sentence or two; they are not being briefed for a quiz. AGENTS.md §3 ("untimed encounters; trivia is incidental, not core"), §4 pillar 4 ("learning is a side effect of presence; information arrives in the world (signs, NPCs, menus), not in pop-ups"), and §7.1 ("the world is the protagonist") all converge on this: **the description should let the player notice what's there, not be told a list.** The deeper history lives in M3 NPC dialogue and M4 journal-note unlocks. The description is the entry point.

For Largo do Carmo specifically, the dual-layer challenge is real: 1755 and 1974 are both load-bearing, and the description has to carry both without enumerating *"earthquake site of 1755 + revolution surrender site of 1974"* (textbook entry — fails §3 register).

The cozy-honest move is to **describe what the player sees** (the roofless church above; the GNR gate with carnations; plane trees and a fountain) and let the historical weight come through the description's tone, not its facts. The player who knows 1755 reads the roofless church as the earthquake; the player who doesn't, sees a roofless church and notes "huh, that's striking" — and then later, M3 dialogue or M4 journal supplies the why. **Both readings are legitimate and the description serves both.**

### Candidate descriptions (3, range from terse to slightly lyrical)

The M1 PR1 prose register is the comparison point. M1 hits "Castelo de São Jorge — a hilltop fortress with five layers of past, partly a 1940s restoration" / "Pensão Estrela do Tejo — a worn pocket pensão tucked into the Baixa Pombalina"-style cadence: short, observational, one or two specific anchors per sentence.

**Candidate A (terse, M1-PR1-cadence):**

> *Largo do Carmo — a plane-tree square below a roofless Gothic church. A fountain in the middle, a working GNR barracks on the south side. Often there are carnations pinned to the gate.*

*Why it works:* every clause is something the player sees. "Roofless Gothic church" carries 1755 without naming it. "Carnations pinned to the gate" carries 1974 without naming it. The reader who knows the history reads both layers; the reader who doesn't, sees a square. No fact is asserted that requires footnoting.

**Candidate B (slightly more breath, mid-cadence):**

> *Largo do Carmo — plane trees, a Pombaline fountain, and a Gothic church that's been roofless since All Saints' Day 1755. The barracks across the square is a working GNR post; the carnations on the gate are usually fresher than they should be.*

*Why it works:* names 1755 explicitly via "All Saints' Day" (which is more evocative than "the earthquake" and also more accurate — All Saints' Day is what made it All Saints' Day, the religious calendar is the reason for the death toll's geography). Carnations-fresher-than-they-should-be is the 1974 hint without naming it; the reader who knows the Carnation Revolution gets the wink.

**Candidate C (most observational, slightly lyrical — closest to the Anthropologist's "Lisboeta sensibility" register from M1 PR1):**

> *Largo do Carmo — a square with a fountain, plane trees for the shade, a roofless church above, and the Guarda Nacional barracks across the way. Locals stop here. So do the buskers. The carnations on the barracks gate are red — and someone, always, has just been by.*

*Why it works:* gives the busking the soft permission ("so do the buskers") that PR8 needs without being directive. "Locals stop here" is the *largo* register — neighborhood-living-room, not monument-frame. "Someone, always, has just been by" is the cozy hint at the carnation cycle without forcing the player to know why.

**Recommendation:** Candidate B is my pick. It carries 1755 explicitly (with the right phrase — *"All Saints' Day"* not *"earthquake"*), it carries 1974 implicitly (carnations + GNR), it stays under 40 words, and it reads as observation. Candidate A is the terser fallback if the FD wants a cleaner two-sentence shape. Candidate C is the most evocative but slightly editorial ("locals stop here") — the Anthropologist will have a better feel for whether that register fits.

### Stratification — what goes in description vs M3 dialogue vs M4 journal

| Layer | Surface | What goes here |
|---|---|---|
| **POI description** | Tap POI marker, read at peek/half snap | What the player sees: roofless church, plane trees, fountain, GNR barracks, carnations. One-sentence-each evocative anchors. **No dates beyond "All Saints' Day 1755" if any.** |
| **M3 NPC dialogue** | Talk to a busker / passerby / shopkeeper | The why: the earthquake congregations, Salgueiro Maia commanding the siege, Celeste Caeiro and the carnations, the *retornados* who came home to a square that now meant democracy. **Specific names, specific dates, specific stories.** Yarn Spinner branches based on relationship value. |
| **M4 journal note** | Auto-populates after visiting, unlocks after dialogue | The synthesis: a paragraph that ties the two layers together — *"This is the square where the regime fell on 25 April 1974, in front of a Gothic church that had been roofless since All Saints' Day 1755. Two ruins, two memorials, one square."* |

**Confidence on this stratification: high.** It mirrors what PR7 established for the azulejo work — the description names a fragment, the M3 dialogue is where Mestra Fernanda tells you *"my grandmother had this panel on her kitchen wall."* PR8 should not break that pattern.

## Topic D — Period detail / signage / material culture

What the player physically encounters in the square. Itemizing for FD substrate:

- **Plane trees** (*plátanos*, *Platanus × acerifolia*) — mature, urban-planted likely 19th–early-20th c. (Pombaline-era squares were typically paved without trees; the 19th-c. Romantic urbanism added the trees). Provides shade. Their seasonal leaf-drop is a visible Lisbon rhythm. **Confidence: scholarly consensus** for the genus and the planting era.
- **Chafariz do Carmo (the fountain)** — Pombaline-era (c. 1771), octagonal basin with central column, mascaron water-spouts. Still flows. **Confidence: well-documented**. Carries a date inscription typically.
- **Cobbled paving** (*calçada portuguesa*) — black-and-white limestone-and-basalt mosaic in geometric patterns. The technique was systematized for public squares from the **1840s onward** (Eusébio Furtado's mid-19th-c. systematization), but the Carmo paving is likely a 20th-c. relay. **Confidence: scholarly consensus** for the technique's history.
- **Café tables** — at least one or two cafés on the square's edges (the *Café Brasileira do Chiado* is around the corner, not on the Largo proper — flag this so descriptions don't conflate). Outdoor seating in season.
- **Quartel do Carmo (GNR barracks)** — south side. Historicist 19th-c. building (the original Carmelite convent buildings adapted into a military barracks in the 19th c. after the 1834 dissolution of religious orders). Heavy wooden gate, **plaque(s)** commemorating 25 April 1974, sometimes carnations pinned to the gate (cyclic, often present, especially around the anniversary). The current GNR honor guard cycle means there's typically uniformed personnel visible at the entrance.
- **Convento do Carmo / Museu Arqueológico façade** — north/west side. Gothic façade with rose window and pointed-arch portal. Survived 1755 structurally; the roof and most of the nave behind did not. **Plaque**: Museu Arqueológico do Carmo entry, hours, 1864 founding date attribution.
- **The Elevador de Santa Justa upper exit** — northeast corner of the square area. **Eiffel-disciple**: not Eiffel himself but **Raoul Mesnier de Ponsard** (Portuguese-French engineer who studied Eiffel's work; sometimes called an Eiffel apprentice in popular sources but the master-apprentice claim is contested in scholarship). **Built 1900–1902, opened 31 August 1902.** Iron lattice tower, ~45m tall, two wood-paneled cabin lifts. Connects the Baixa (Rua de Santa Justa) to the Largo do Carmo via an upper walkway. **The upper-platform observation deck (additional ticket) looks down on Largo do Carmo from above** — many tourists arrive at the Largo via this route. **Confidence: well-documented** for dates and engineer; **active scholarly correction** on the "Eiffel apprentice" claim — Mesnier de Ponsard studied Eiffel's *work*, but direct apprenticeship is a popular embellishment. If the description ever names this elevator, prefer "in the iron-lattice family of Eiffel-era engineering" over "by an apprentice of Eiffel."
- **Salgueiro Maia statue / commemoration** — there is **no large standalone statue of Salgueiro Maia in Largo do Carmo itself**, to my knowledge. There is a **plaque to him** somewhere on the square's perimeter (commemorating his role in the surrender). Larger commemoration of him exists elsewhere in Portugal (notably Santarém, his unit's origin). **Flag for FD**: if PR8 wants to reference a Salgueiro Maia memorial, verify current site dressing via Mapillary; do not invent a statue.
- **Ambient sound register**: tourists, plane-tree birds (sparrows, *pardais*), the GNR honor-guard footsteps when the watch changes, the elevator's mechanical hum if the wind is right, occasional car traffic on Rua da Trindade. *Buskers when present.* The square is calmer than Rossio or Chiado proper because it's **off the main tourist circulation** (you have to climb up to it; the elevator brings some, but not the majority).
- **Smells**: linden-and-plane-tree pollen in season, exhaust at edges, food from nearby cafés.

## Topic E — Cross-cutting historical flags

### Colonial-legacy ADR position

AGENTS.md §15 states a colonial-legacy ADR is required **before any Belém POI** is written. The question for PR8 is whether *Largo do Carmo* triggers the ADR or whether the ADR is pre-empted by Carmo just naming-the-history-without-the-ADR.

**My position: PR8 does NOT trigger the colonial-legacy ADR. PR8 names the 25 April 1974 / Carnation Revolution layer; the ADR is Belém-triggered.**

Reasoning:
- The §15 wording is specifically *"a colonial-legacy ADR must exist before any Belém POI"* — Belém-triggered, not 1974-triggered. Belém is where the *colonial-empire-as-adventure-flavored-backdrop* trap (M1 PR1 Anthropologist's stereotype-traps list) lives — the *Padrão dos Descobrimentos* (1960 Estado Novo monument), the *Mosteiro dos Jerónimos*, the Tower of Belém, all valorize the 15th–16th c. maritime expansion. That is the ADR's substantive territory.
- 25 April 1974 / Carnation Revolution / decolonization aftermath is **the antidote to** the Belém register, not its equivalent. The Carmo description names "the regime fell here" and "the wars ended"; the Belém description (forthcoming, post-ADR) will need to name "this is where the wars started," 470 years earlier. ### Temporal-arc flag from M1 PR1

The M1 PR1 surprise was that the 5-POI shortlist accidentally traced Iron Age → 2016 in five layers. The lesson surfaced was *"future Lisbon POI additions should pick for **temporal gaps**"* — Belém (1500s maritime), Carmo (1755 earthquake memorial), Alfama (medieval-Moorish density). PR8 is **adding two layers in one POI**: 1755 and 1974. This is unusual.

**My position:** this is good. It compresses; it doesn't proliferate. A 6-POI Lisbon adding two temporal layers in one POI is a tighter information density than adding two POIs each with one layer. The square is unusually rich because of historical contingency (Caetano happened to flee to *this* GNR barracks; the Carmelites happened to lose *this* church) — and the project is honest to the city by representing Largo do Carmo as the dual-memorial it actually is. If the future M3+ Alfama POI adds the medieval-Moorish layer and the future Belém POI adds the maritime-empire layer, the temporal arc through Lisbon will be: Iron Age + Roman + Moorish + medieval-royal (Castelo); medieval-Moorish-density (Alfama); maritime-1500s + decolonization (Belém — the ADR will demand both layers); 1755 earthquake + 1974 revolution (Carmo); Pombaline 18th c. (Pensão Estrela do Tejo, Mercado da Ribeira); 19th-c. iron-and-glass (Mercado da Ribeira, Elevador de Santa Justa); 1942–1965–2016 (Aeroporto Humberto Delgado). **That's a temporally complete cross-section by M3 — a remarkable thing for a 7–8 POI Lisbon.**

The PR7 azulejo-restoration aesthetics (recovering *real* azulejo from Estado-Novo-instrumentalized institutional buildings) and the PR8 Largo do Carmo (where Estado Novo politics ended in ~17:30 on a Thursday afternoon) are two ends of the same arc. **A player who completes both encounters will, without being told, have a coherent picture of "the regime that ran Portugal until 1974."** That is the §4 pillar 4 ("learning is a side effect of presence") working at maximum efficacy.

## Topic F — POI alternatives

If the Anthropologist (parallel review) recommends a different POI than Largo do Carmo as the busking site, my historical position on the alternatives:

- **Praça do Rossio (Praça D. Pedro IV):** 1755 destruction + Pombaline rebuild + 1908 regicide site. **Historical layers**: as deep as Largo do Carmo, possibly deeper. The 1908 regicide of King Carlos I and Crown Prince Luís Filipe by the Carbonária was on **Praça do Comércio**, not Rossio — clarifying because popular sources sometimes misplace it. Rossio carries the **autos-da-fé of the Inquisition** (15th–18th c., the burnings happened here when the Inquisition's tribunal was based at the Palácio dos Estaus on the north end), the **Theatro Nacional D. Maria II** (1840s, on the site of the demolished Palácio dos Estaus — the regime literally built the national theatre on the site of the Inquisition's headquarters, an 1840s liberal-romantic gesture), and the **Pombaline rebuilt facades**. Heavy historical weight. **Busking-feasibility**: yes, real busking happens, often. **But:** Rossio doesn't have the *single-image memorial-anchor* of Largo do Carmo's roofless church + carnation gate. It's more dispersed; harder to carry in a 40-word description. **My position: Largo do Carmo is the better choice if the project is keeping a busking POI singular; Rossio is the better choice if the project ever wants a "civic ceremonial life" POI later.** I would not push back if the Anthropologist preferred Rossio for busking-density reasons, but I would flag that the historical weight at Rossio is harder to compress.

- **Praça do Comércio (Terreiro do Paço):** 1755 destruction + royal-palace-loss (the Paço da Ribeira was destroyed in the quake, never rebuilt) + 1908 regicide (King Carlos I and Crown Prince Luís Filipe assassinated by Manuel Buíça and Alfredo Costa as the royal carriage entered the square from the Tagus side; the brother Manuel II became king and would lose the throne in 1910) + Pombaline grid frontage + 25 April 1974 (some MFA forces moved through here). Massive historical weight. **But:** monumental, formal, less *largo*-register, less daily-Lisboeta. Buskers do work here but it has more of a ceremonial-square feel than a neighborhood-square feel. **My position: NOT the right choice for the busking POI.** Praça do Comércio is the *Castelo de São Jorge*-equivalent for civic / monumental Lisbon — too big, too formal. Save it for a future "monumental Lisbon" POI that anchors the riverfront-as-empire-portal narrative (which connects to the Belém / colonial-legacy ADR). If the Anthropologist suggests it, I push back: not this POI, this purpose.

- **Largo de São Domingos:** 1755 partial damage to the Igreja de São Domingos (which structurally survived 1755 with damage; **catastrophically burned in a 1959 fire**, was reconstructed in the 1990s with the burn-marks left visible — the interior is a remarkable record of fire); **Inquisition autos-da-fé were held here from 1540 onward** (the Largo's east end was where the burnings were staged); **2008 Holocaust-style memorial to the 1506 Lisbon Massacre** (the *Massacre dos Judeus de Lisboa* — a Christian mob killed ~2,000 New Christian Jews here over three days in April 1506; the memorial is a wall plaque + memorial stone with quotations from Amos Oz, José Saramago, and others); **active *ginja-bar* register today** (*A Ginjinha* on the square, founded 1840, the original sour-cherry-liqueur-shot bar); **Cape Verdean / São Toméan presence today** (the square is an informal gathering point for the Lusophone-African community in central Lisbon, especially in the late afternoon). **Real busking happens.** **My position:** Largo de São Domingos is **historically as rich as Largo do Carmo** but in a different register — the 1506 massacre is genuinely heavy (and the project would need to handle it carefully, per §9.3 sacred/sensitive content), the autos-da-fé-then-fire-then-reconstruction layering is extraordinary, the contemporary Lusophone-African presence directly addresses the M1 PR1 flag *"Lisbon's Africanness is absent and that's a problem to solve by M3."* **If the Anthropologist pushes for São Domingos for cultural-busking reasons (especially the Lusophone-African angle), I would seriously consider aligning** — but with a caveat: **the 1506 massacre + autos-da-fé layering is heavier sacred/sensitive content than the 1755 + 1974 layering at Largo do Carmo**, and a busking POI's description can't carry that weight without violating the §3 cozy-anti-trivia register. The 25 April carnation symbolism only attaches to Largo do Carmo, and that's a meaningful loss if we move. **My net position: Largo do Carmo is the right busking POI for the project's M2 ship; Largo de São Domingos is the right busking POI for a future M3+ that's specifically designed to land the Lusophone-African Lisbon presence.** If the Anthropologist proposes São Domingos for M2 PR8, I'd ask for the project to commit to *both* (Carmo at PR8, São Domingos as a future POI in the M3+ work) rather than substituting one for the other.

**Decision summary for FD:** **Hold Largo do Carmo as the M2 PR8 busking POI.** The Anthropologist's parallel review may surface busking-convention or stereotype-trap concerns I haven't anticipated; if they push back, the conversation should be "is São Domingos a swap or an addition?", not "is Carmo wrong?" The 1755 + 1974 dual-memorial weight is genuinely unique to Largo do Carmo; substituting loses both layers in exchange for whatever the alternative gains.

## Sources

Portuguese-language scholarship preferred per §9.3 ("sources from the culture, not just about it"). Citations are scholarly-consensus references; specific volumes are listed where I can name them; some institutional sources are pointers to public archives.

**On the 1755 earthquake:**
- **França, José-Augusto** — *Lisboa Pombalina e o Iluminismo* (3rd ed., Bertrand, 1987). The standard reference for Pombaline reconstruction; the Carmo abandonment is documented here.
- **Maxwell, Kenneth** — *Pombal, Paradox of the Enlightenment* (Cambridge UP, 1995). Anglophone but tightly-engaged with Portuguese sources; covers the Pombal-vs-Carmelite ideological angle of the post-quake reconstruction priorities.
- **Mendonça, Joaquim Joseph Moreira de** — *História Universal dos Terramotos* (1758). **Primary source.** The Lisbon Royal Academy's first systematic earthquake survey, published three years after the event. Available in facsimile.
- **Buescu, Helena Carvalhão & Cordeiro, Gonçalo (eds.)** — *O Grande Terramoto de Lisboa: Ficar Diferente* (Gradiva, 2005). 250th-anniversary multi-author volume; current scholarship.
- **Paice, Edward** — *Wrath of God: The Great Lisbon Earthquake of 1755* (Quercus, 2008). Anglophone narrative history; useful for the death-toll-debate framing and the post-quake political consequences.
- **Câmara, Maria Alexandra Trindade Gago da** — already cited in PR7 for 18th-c. azulejo; her work on Pombaline architectural history is also relevant.

**On the Convento do Carmo specifically:**
- **Associação dos Arqueólogos Portugueses** — institutional publications and the Museu Arqueológico do Carmo's own catalogues. Public-archive material; the museum's website (https://museuarqueologicodocarmo.pt/) is the canonical institutional reference.
- **Pereira, Paulo (ed.)** — *História da Arte Portuguesa* (Círculo de Leitores, 1995, 3 vols.). The standard general reference on Portuguese art history; the Carmo's pre-1755 Gothic significance is documented in vol. 1.
- **Silva, José Custódio Vieira da** — research on Portuguese Gothic architecture; multiple monographs on medieval ecclesiastical building.

**On the 25 April 1974 / Carnation Revolution:**
- **Maxwell, Kenneth** — *The Making of Portuguese Democracy* (Cambridge UP, 1995). Standard Anglophone reference; tightly sourced to Portuguese archives.
- **Pinto, António Costa** — *O Estado Novo Português: Política, Ideologia e Economia* and the broader body of his work in Portuguese (ICS — Instituto de Ciências Sociais, Universidade de Lisboa). The current scholarly voice on the Estado Novo and its end.
- **Rezola, Maria Inácia** — *25 de Abril: Mitos de uma Revolução* (Esfera dos Livros, 2007) and her Centro de Documentação 25 de Abril work. The contemporary Portuguese scholarly voice specifically on the Revolution and its mythology.
- **Centro de Documentação 25 de Abril (Universidade de Coimbra)** — https://www1.ci.uc.pt/cd25a/ — the canonical institutional archive; primary documents, oral histories, photographs from the day. Public-access.
- **Antunes, José Freire** — *Os Spínolas — Cumplicidade e Ruptura* and his other work on the MFA / Spínola axis.
- **Maia, Salgueiro** — *Capitão de Abril: Histórias da Guerra do Ultramar e do 25 de Abril* (Editorial Notícias, 1994). Salgueiro Maia's own memoir, posthumous-adjacent. **Primary source.**
- **RTP archive footage** — the 25 April 1974 RTP broadcasts and field reportage are publicly available via the RTP Arquivos online portal. The Largo do Carmo siege footage is canonical.

**On the *retornados* and decolonization:**
- **Pires, Rui Pena** — *Migrações e Integração: Teoria e Aplicações à Sociedade Portuguesa* (Celta, 2003) and his subsequent work. The standard reference on Portuguese migration patterns including the *retornados*.
- **Lubkemann, Stephen C.** — Anglophone but rigorous; multiple articles on the *retornados* identity question.
- **Ovelha, Magaly** and **Peralta, Elsa** — recent research on the *retornados* memory and the post-2000 reckoning. Available via the *Institute of Contemporary History* (UNL).

**On the Elevador de Santa Justa:**
- **Câmara Municipal de Lisboa** institutional history; multiple guidebooks. The Eiffel-apprentice claim is widespread in popular sources but unsupported in primary documentation; Mesnier de Ponsard's training is documented in his own engineering papers and in Carris (the Lisbon transit operator, current owner of the Elevador) institutional history.
