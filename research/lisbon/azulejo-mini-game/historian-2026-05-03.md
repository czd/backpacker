## Historian M2 PR7 azulejo discovery

### Topic A — Panel pattern historical accuracy

**A1 (17th c. polychrome — *azulejo de tapete*):**

- **Era boundaries.** *Azulejo de tapete* (carpet azulejo) is conventionally dated **c. 1600–1690**, with the densest production window **c. 1640–1680** under the post-Restauração regime of João IV and his successors. The naming refers to the tile panel's resemblance to woven Persian/Anatolian carpets — repeating geometric modules across a wall-sized field, not figurative scenes. The earlier 16th-century Hispano-Moresque tradition (*aresta* / *cuerda seca* tiles imported from Seville and Talavera, c. 1500–1560, e.g. the Sintra Royal Palace's *Sala dos Árabes* and *Sala dos Brasões* dado) is a separate predecessor, not the same tradition. The transition out of *tapete* and into figurative blue-and-white starts under Pedro II (c. 1690) and is essentially complete by 1715.
- **Geographic / institutional anchors.** Strong surviving panels at: **Igreja de São Vicente de Fora** (the 17th c. dado work, distinct from the 18th c. blue-and-white *Lusíadas* and *La Fontaine* fables in the upper cloister); **Igreja-Convento da Madre de Deus** (lower-cloister polychrome panels, c. 1660–1680, *before* the famous blue-and-white refit); **Igreja de Santo Antão-o-Novo** (now part of Hospital de São José) for *tapete*-style work; the **Quinta da Bacalhôa** at Azeitão for the older 16th c. Hispano-Moresque + early *istoriato* hybrid (a cousin tradition, *not* the same as *tapete*); **Igreja de Marvila** and **Sé de Évora** for regional examples outside Lisbon. UI Designer should anchor to the São Vicente / Madre de Deus dados — those are the canonical *tapete* survivors.
- **Production context.** Lisbon was the dominant production center by mid-17th c. — the earlier reliance on Sevillian and Talavera imports collapsed during the Iberian Union (1580–1640) and especially after 1640 when Portuguese workshops scaled up to meet domestic demand. Workshops clustered around **Santos-o-Velho** and **Anjos** parishes (both in modern Lisbon). Named masters from the era are mostly anonymous workshop output — the tradition was guild-based and signatures are rare for *tapete*. **Marçal de Matos** and **Francisco de Matos** are documented late-16th / early-17th c. names but their attributions are debated; see Meco below.
- **Material / technical reality.** Tin-glazed earthenware (*faiança*) — red clay body, white opacifying glaze (tin oxide + lead silicate), pigments painted onto the unfired glaze (*maiolica* technique, from the Italian tradition by way of Antwerp). Fired once at **~950–1000°C**. Standard tile size **14×14 cm** by the 17th c. (the older 16th c. tiles ran ~13.5 cm or so; the dimension was not strictly standardized until the 18th c.). Pigments: cobalt blue, copper green, manganese purple/brown, antimony yellow, iron-oxide ochre — the polychrome palette. Painted **freehand or by stencil** (*estampilha* — pricked paper pattern, charcoal pounce); the *tapete* repeating modules made stencils economical for large fields. Mortar was lime-based (*argamassa de cal*), often with marble dust, applied to a brick or rubble wall. **Wear pattern:** efflorescence (white salt blooms from masonry behind), pigment loss especially in yellows and greens (less stable than cobalt), edge chipping where mortar cracked, cobalt strike-through on the few blue accents. The yellow specifically tends to "burn out" first — UI Designer can use this for authentic wear.

**A2 (18th c. blue-and-white figurative):**

- **Era boundaries.** Portuguese blue-and-white figurative azulejo runs **c. 1690–1780**, peaking in two distinct phases: the **Ciclo dos Mestres / Master Cycles c. 1690–1725** (large-scale figurative narrative panels, often signed) and the **Pombaline / Joanino phase c. 1755–1780** (post-earthquake reconstruction, Rococo lightness, often more decorative and less narrative). The fade is gradual — by 1780 polychrome returns under neoclassicism (the *Real Fábrica do Rato*, founded 1767, drives this), and by 1820 the industrial *azulejo de fábrica* replaces hand-painted work for most domestic applications. The "blue-and-white" stylistic dominance is genuinely mid-period — bracketed by polychrome on both ends — and that itself is a useful corrective to the gift-shop assumption that azulejo = blue-and-white-forever.
- **Geographic / institutional anchors.** The Master Cycle survivors: **Igreja-Convento da Madre de Deus** upper cloister (the *Vida de Santa Clara* panels, c. 1700–1710, attributed to **António de Oliveira Bernardes**); **Igreja de São Lourenço de Almancil** in the Algarve (c. 1730 — pre-1755, so untouched by the earthquake, often cited as the most spectacular intact narrative-cycle interior); **Palácio dos Marqueses de Fronteira** in Benfica (the *Sala das Batalhas* panels c. 1670 are technically polychrome-into-blue transition; the gardens have late-17th c. blue-and-white); **Igreja de São Vicente de Fora** upper cloister (the *La Fontaine fables* panels, post-1740). Pombaline-era anchors: **Igreja de Santa Catarina**, **Igreja dos Anjos** rebuild, the surviving panels in many Baixa staircases. UI Designer's strongest visual reference is São Lourenço de Almancil if you want pristine narrative work; Madre de Deus if you want Lisbon-located.
- **Production context.** Lisbon workshops clustered around the **Bairro do Anjos** and later **Rato** (after the 1767 royal factory). Named masters with documented work: **António de Oliveira Bernardes** (c. 1660–1732) and his son **Policarpo de Oliveira Bernardes** (1695–1778) — the most prolific signed master cycle. **Manuel dos Santos**, **Valentim de Almeida**, **Bartolomeu Antunes**, **Nicolau de Freitas**. The *Mestres dos Macacos* (lit. "Masters of the Monkeys") is an attribution of convenience for an anonymous workshop active c. 1715–1730 whose panels feature satirical scenes including monkeys aping human activities — they're a real attribution tradition, not a single named workshop, and scholarship is divided on whether the corpus is one or several hands. Imports of Dutch Delftware tiles continued through the late 17th c. (Maria Francisca de Sabóia commissioned Delft panels for the Palácio das Necessidades) but by ~1700 Portuguese production fully eclipsed imports.
- **Material / technical reality.** Same tin-glazed earthenware as polychrome, but cobalt-only painting (cobalt is the most fire-stable pigment and survives glaze firing most consistently — partly *why* the period went monochrome). Same ~14×14 cm tile size, increasingly standardized. The figurative work used **estampilha** (pounce-patterning) for repeated figures and freehand for unique narrative elements. **Brushwork distinction:** Master Cycle panels show confident *aguada* (wash) shading with cobalt-diluted-in-water — visible on close inspection as gradient brush passes. Post-1755 Pombaline work is often flatter, more linear, less wash. **Wear pattern:** cobalt is durable but the white glaze yellows over time, mortar joints show extensive *eflorescência* especially on north-facing church walls, and many panels have been "rearranged" during 19th-c. restorations (so misaligned figures across grout lines is itself historically authentic — a tile out of order isn't always damage, sometimes it's a Victorian-era amateur reinstall).

### Topic B — Workshop framing

**Recommended option: Option 4 (new) — A working master-restorer's *atelier* in Alfama, framed within the *Reabilitação Urbana* movement, with the Madre de Deus convent / Museu Nacional do Azulejo positioned as the **training/reference institution** the master is connected to but not employed by.**

This is a synthesis of options 1, 2, and 3 that's historically more defensible than any single one alone:

- **Why not pure Option 2 (museum-as-employer):** the Museu Nacional do Azulejo (founded 1965 within the Convento da Madre de Deus, designated *Museu Nacional* in 1980) does run a conservation and restoration *laboratório*, but they don't hire street-level apprentices through walk-in arrangements. The framing "the museum hires you" is a fiction that misrepresents how a real cultural-heritage institution operates. It would also concentrate the entire azulejo experience inside one tourist-target museum, which is exactly the gift-shop register the Anthropologist flagged against.
- **Why not pure Option 3 (Alfama master, no museum tie):** historically thin — Alfama was *not* the historic azulejo workshop neighborhood (Anjos, Santos-o-Velho, and later Rato were). Putting a workshop in Alfama is plausible for a *modern* restorer's atelier (the bairro's medieval-Moorish density and surviving azulejo'd facades make it a natural restoration *site*), but framing it as a traditional workshop neighborhood would be inaccurate.
- **Why not pure Option 1 (Mercado da Ribeira pickup):** the Ribeira market has no historical or contemporary connection to azulejo trade. It's a fish/produce market and now also a food court. The pickup-point logic is fictional set-dressing that doesn't carry cultural weight.

The **Reabilitação Urbana** anchor solves all three problems at once. The historical scaffolding: post-1974 Lisbon faced massive housing-stock decay especially in Alfama, Mouraria, Bairro Alto, and Madragoa. The **GTH (Gabinete Técnico de Habitação)**, founded 1985, and its successor **SRU — Sociedade de Reabilitação Urbana** Lisboa Ocidental and Lisboa Central (founded 2004, merged into **Lisboa Ocidental SRU + EPUL** restructured into the current municipal company structure c. 2009–2015) ran systematic facade and building restoration programs. A specific azulejo-recovery program — the **AZULEJO project** of the Câmara Municipal de Lisboa (c. 2013) — explicitly targeted theft-prevention and restoration of facade azulejos following the 2010s wave of azulejo theft (the *Polícia Judiciária* ran a real "azulejo task force" because 18th/19th c. panels were being pried off facades and trafficked to Northern European antique markets). The **Banco do Azulejo** (a CML/Museu Nacional do Azulejo joint inventory of recovered/donated tiles, available to property owners restoring historical buildings) is the real-world institution that legitimizes "I'm fitting historical fragments back into walls."

So the recommended frame: a small *atelier de restauro* in Alfama (or Madragoa — also defensible, less tourist-overrun), run by a fictional master-restorer who **trained at the Museu Nacional do Azulejo's conservation programme** (or via the IEFP heritage-trades pathway) and **works in coordination with the SRU and the Banco do Azulejo**. The player picks up panels at the atelier, restores them, returns them. The Madre de Deus / Museu Nacional do Azulejo is named as backstory anchor (M3 dialogue hook: "I trained there in the 90s..."), not as the workshop itself.

**Anchoring institution / movement:**

The *Reabilitação Urbana* movement is the load-bearing real-world hook and is more textured than a single named program. Specifically: the post-2008 financial-crisis acceleration of urban renovation in Lisbon's historic core, partly funded by Portugal's *Programa Operacional Regional de Lisboa* (EU structural funds), partly by the **IFRRU 2020** instrument, partly by tourism-driven private investment. The dark side — and the project should be aware — is that *Reabilitação Urbana* is not innocent: it's deeply entangled with the gentrification crisis (the *Lei das Rendas* of 2012, the Golden Visa programme of 2012–2023, AirBnB conversions). The same restoration that recovers an 18th c. panel often also displaces the 80-year-old tenant who'd lived in the building since 1962. This is the M3-NPC complexity the Build Log already flagged ("at minimum: one NPC whose family was pushed out of the bairro should exist"). The fictional master-restorer can carry this honestly — they restore tiles for a living *and* the city they're restoring is one their own kids can't afford to live in. That's true to the real Lisbon and avoids cozy-washing.

Real master-restorers worth knowing as backstory inspiration (do **not** name the player's master after any of them — fictional naming per ADR-003 logic): **Lurdes Esteves** (Museu Nacional do Azulejo conservator, decades of work); **João Manuel Mimoso** (LNEC researcher, the major scientific reference on azulejo materials degradation); **Teresa Campos** (CML cultural-heritage technician). The fictional master can have "studied with someone like X" in M3 dialogue without claiming a real name.

### Topic D — Period detail for UI Designer

**Workshop tools:**
- *Pincel de pelo de marta* (sable-hair brushes), graduated sizes — the smallest for line work, larger for *aguada* wash. Visibly worn handles.
- Glass jars or small *gibões* (ceramic cups) of cobalt oxide pigment, in dry-powder and water-suspended forms; for restoration, also manganese, antimony, copper-green if matching polychrome.
- *Estampilha* — pricked-paper stencils stacked on a table; charcoal pounce bag (a small cloth bag of powdered charcoal, dabbed through the perforations).
- *Espátula* (small flat trowel) and *colher de pedreiro* (mason's trowel) for *argamassa de cal* (lime mortar) — note the *cal hidráulica natural* type for traditional restoration, never modern Portland cement on historical work.
- *Cravadeira* / small chisel and a tile-cutting wheel for trimming replacement tiles to fit irregular gaps.
- A small electric or gas-fired *forno* (kiln) for re-firing replacement tiles — but most ateliers send pieces out to specialist kilns rather than firing on-site, so the kiln may be implied (a clay-stained shelf of tiles awaiting pickup) rather than rendered.
- Reference shelves: bound volumes of *Azulejaria em Portugal* (Santos Simões), photo archives, pattern catalogs.
- *Banco de azulejos* / sorting trays — wooden trays divided into compartments, holding salvaged historical tile fragments awaiting reuse. This is the texture of working from the *Banco do Azulejo* recovery system.

**Original wall context options:**
- Lower-cloister dado of a Lisbon convent church (most common for *tapete*) — sets the panel ~1m above floor, capped at ~2.5m height; mortar-staining at the base from centuries of floor washing.
- Stairway / *escadaria* of a Pombaline townhouse — narrow vertical panels following the stair, often 18th c. blue-and-white.
- Hospital/asylum wall (e.g., Hospital de São José ex-Santo Antão) — institutional context, often heavy soot/smoke patina from centuries of indoor lighting.
- Palace garden retaining wall (*muro*) — outdoor exposure, water staining, frost-cracking on north faces.
- Facade dado of an Alfama or Madragoa apartment building — 19th-early-20th c. vernacular reuse of older tile traditions; weathered, often partial because of facade theft.
- Choose **one** for the visual reference per panel. For the polychrome: lower-cloister dado (Madre de Deus or São Vicente de Fora). For the blue-and-white: a stairway in a Pombaline rebuild, mid-staircase fragment.

**"Missing tiles" damage scenarios — cracked-edge aesthetic guidance:**
- **1755 earthquake damage** (specifically for any 18th c. panel in a pre-Pombaline location) — clean diagonal fracture lines through tile fields, many tiles displaced rather than destroyed; a real restoration scenario for any panel from a building that survived the quake structurally but lost its dado.
- **Moisture / salt damage** from rising damp — efflorescence rings around missing tiles, gradual loss-of-adhesion rather than violent break; the most common real cause.
- **20th c. infrastructure work** — electrical conduit runs cut through 18th c. panels in the 1950s–1970s; classic rectangular gaps where a tile sequence was hacked out for a wire run, then abandoned. This is genuinely common in Portuguese building stock.
- **Theft (post-2000)** — clean pry-bar gaps, mortar-back exposed in a regular grid; the *Polícia Judiciária* azulejo-theft wave c. 2010–2015 is recent enough to be active narrative. The recovered tiles enter the *Banco do Azulejo*; the master is restoring panels using **recovered-from-theft** tiles. This is an honest, non-trivializing, contemporary frame.
- **Wartime damage** — *not* relevant for Lisbon (Portugal was neutral in WWII; no urban combat in modern era). Avoid.
- **Vandalism (graffiti / impact)** — a real but uncozy cause; better to skip for the mini-game framing.

UI Designer's most evocative single: **theft-recovery + 1755 fracture + rising-damp loss** as a layered scenario for the 18th c. panel; **convent dado long-term moisture loss** for the polychrome.

### Topic E — Cross-cutting historical flags

- **Pombaline reconstruction context (post-1755):** any 18th c. blue-and-white panel set in a Lisbon location is statistically more likely from a **rebuilt** church than an original, because most central-Lisbon parish churches lost their interiors on 1 November 1755 (All Saints' Day morning Mass — congregations were gathered, candles were lit; the death toll concentrated in churches partly explains the post-quake religious crisis). Panels in **Madre de Deus, São Vicente de Fora, Almancil**, and Belém survived because of geography (Madre de Deus is east of the impact center; Belém is west; Almancil is in the Algarve). M3 dialogue hook: the master's panel-of-the-day might be "from a pre-quake church that survived" or "from a Pombaline rebuild that's now itself 270 years old." Don't conflate the two. If the project ever frames a panel as from a destroyed-and-rebuilt church (e.g., **Igreja de Santa Catarina**), the description should land that the wall the player's master is restoring is itself part of the city's reconstruction memory.

- **Estado Novo heritage politics & the *Reabilitação Urbana* movement:** the Salazar-era restoration of Castelo de São Jorge (M1 PR1 flag) was DGEMN's nation-building program — partly real conservation, partly invented-medievalism dressed as recovery. The *Reabilitação Urbana* movement post-1985 is **structurally different** (democratic-era, EU-funded, scientifically-disciplined via LNEC) and does not carry the same ideological baggage; *however*, the **Estado Novo's azulejo politics** are a separate concern: the regime restored and commissioned massive figurative panels for public buildings (notably the **Aeroporto da Portela** original 1942 terminal had Estado Novo panels; many Lisbon metro stations of the 1959–1972 first-generation set, e.g., **Restauradores**, have panels from the era; some carry didactic-nationalist iconography). The metro station program continued democratically post-1974 — most metro azulejo most travelers see is the **post-1988 expansion** (Maria Keil's earlier work + a generation of post-Carnation-Revolution artists like Júlio Pomar, Vieira da Silva at Cidade Universitária, Rolando Sá Nogueira, Sá Nogueira). **Net flag for PR7:** the master's panels can come from churches, convents, palaces, or pre-1974 institutional buildings; if the project ever surfaces *which institution* a panel came from, avoid Aeroporto-original or pre-1959 metro stations unless the dialogue frames the politics honestly. Safer source institutions: any pre-1755 ecclesiastical building, any 18th c. private palace, any post-1988 metro station's azulejo lineage (which are themselves art commissions worth honoring rather than restoring-as-relic).

- **Colonial empire's role in the polychrome tradition:**
  - The **Hispano-Moresque influence** that seeded the 16th-c. Iberian tradition came via the Iberian-Moorish heritage of Al-Andalus and the Almohad/Nasrid traditions — this is *not* a colonial-era influence, it predates the Portuguese maritime empire. Distinguish carefully: *Mudéjar* / *Mozárabe* tile work is medieval Iberian, not colonial-extracted.
  - **Pigment trade routes do involve the empire.** Cobalt for the blue came largely from Saxony / Iran / China via Antwerp and Amsterdam (not directly colonial). But: **Brazilian gold** (post-1690s Minas Gerais strikes) financed the João V building boom that commissioned the Master Cycle panels — so the 18th c. blue-and-white "golden age" is materially funded by extracted-Brazilian-gold and the slave economy that produced it. **Manganese** for the brown/purple of polychrome panels was mined in Portugal itself (Beira region) but later supplemented from colonial sources. **Antimony** for yellow had complex sourcing including Ottoman territories. **No direct material from Portuguese Africa or Asia** in the pigment stack itself; the colonial-empire link is **economic** (the gold that paid for the panels), not **chemical** (the pigments in them). M3 dialogue hook for the master: *"This panel was paid for by Brazilian gold. The artist who painted it never asked where the money came from"* — honest, not preachy.

- **M3 NPC potential — historical scaffolding for the master's backstory:**
  - **Born c. 1960** (66 in 2026): grew up in late-Estado-Novo Lisbon, was a teenager during the Carnation Revolution (25 April 1974). The *retornados* arrival of 1975–1976 was a defining moment of their adolescence.
  - **Trained c. 1980–1990:** plausible paths include the IEFP heritage-trades program, the SOAJ (Sociedade Operários da Arte do Azulejo) tradition where it survived, or apprenticeship at a still-active *fábrica* (Viúva Lamego, Sant'Anna — both real, both still operating). The Museu Nacional do Azulejo's conservation laboratory (under the leadership of conservators like João Manuel Mimoso at LNEC) was a possible reference point but *not* a formal training institution in the apprenticeship sense.
  - **Career arc:** the *Reabilitação Urbana* boom of the 2000s–2010s gave their generation steady work after a lean 1990s; the 2010s azulejo-theft wave changed the nature of the work from "restore in place" to "recover and reinstall." The 2020s gentrification crisis is the current contradiction they live in.
  - **Voice register:** Lisboeta working-class with technical vocabulary — they know the difference between *aguada* and *cobalto seco*, they call cobalt *"o azul"* not *"o cobalto"* in casual speech, they curse mildly about *cal hidráulica* shortages, they remember the bairro before AirBnB. Not an academic; a tradesperson with a craftsperson's specific knowledge.
  - **Single most evocative dialogue hook:** *"My grandmother had this panel on her kitchen wall. When the building was sold, the new owners ripped it out — they didn't know it was Bernardes-school. I bought it back at auction. Now I rebuild it for someone who'll keep it."* — anchors continuity, loss, restoration, and the gentrification contradiction in one piece of personal history.
